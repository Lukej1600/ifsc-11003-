The history of each mars rover

Sojourner (1997) Landing as part of the Mars Pathfinder mission, Sojourner was the first wheeled vehicle to roam another planet. About the size of a microwave oven, this tiny six-wheeled scout proved that mobile exploration was possible. It operated for about three months, analyzing rocks near its landing site at Ares Vallis and sending back thousands of images that captivated the world. Spirit & Opportunity (2004) These "twin" rovers were designed to search for evidence of past water. Spirit landed in Gusev Crater and worked for six years before getting stuck in soft sand in 2010. Opportunity, landing on the opposite side of the planet, became a marathon runner; it operated for nearly 15 years and covered over 28 miles (45 km). Both rovers revolutionized our understanding of Mars as a once-wet world, with Opportunity finally falling silent during a massive global dust storm in 2018. Curiosity (2012–Present) Much larger and more advanced, Curiosity is a nuclear-powered mobile lab the size of an SUV. It landed in Gale Crater using a dramatic "sky crane" maneuver. Its primary mission was to determine if Mars ever had an environment capable of supporting microbial life. Over a decade later, it is still active, climbing Mount Sharp and discovering organic molecules and evidence of ancient freshwater lakes. +1 Zhurong (2021–2022) China’s first Mars rover, Zhurong, landed in Utopia Planitia as part of the Tianwen-1 mission. It was designed to study the planet's soil, magnetic field, and internal structure. The solar-powered rover exceeded its original 90-day mission, operating for nearly a year before entering a planned hibernation during the Martian winter. However, it was later confirmed that dust buildup on its panels likely prevented it from waking back up. Perseverance (2021–Present) NASA’s latest rover, nicknamed "Percy," is currently exploring Jezero Crater—an ancient river delta. While it looks like Curiosity, its mission is even more ambitious: searching for actual signs of ancient life and collecting rock samples to be returned to Earth by a future mission. It also brought along Ingenuity, a small helicopter that proved powered flight is possible in the thin Martian atmosphere.
