Frequently asked questions

Questions regarding Mars rovers often focus on the logistical challenges of operating a vehicle on a distant world and the specific technology required to keep them running. One of the most common inquiries is how these robots are controlled; because radio signals take between 5 and 20 minutes to travel between Earth and Mars, rovers cannot be "joy-sticked" in real-time and must rely on pre-programmed commands and autonomous hazard-avoidance systems. People also frequently ask about their power sources; while older rovers like Spirit and Opportunity relied on solar panels (which were vulnerable to dust accumulation), newer models like Curiosity and Perseverance use Multi-Mission Radioisotope Thermoelectric Generators (MMRTGs) that convert heat from decaying plutonium into electricity, allowing them to work through the night and during dust storms. Finally, a common point of curiosity is what happens to the rovers when their missions end—currently, they remain on the Martian surface as permanent artifacts, as there is no existing technology to bring them back home yet.
