A brief history

Since the dawn of the Sojourner mission in 1997, NASA’s Mars exploration program has transformed our understanding of the Red Planet from a distant, frozen mystery into a world of ancient riverbeds and geological wonder. These robotic pioneers—Spirit, Opportunity, Curiosity, and Perseverance—serve as our eyes and ears on the Martian surface, braving extreme temperatures and treacherous dust storms to seek signs of past microbial life. Each rover is a mobile laboratory, equipped with advanced spectroscopic tools and high-definition cameras that beam breathtaking panoramas back to Earth, bridging the 140-million-mile gap between our two worlds. As they drill into ancient lakebeds and collect rock samples for future return to Earth, these rovers are not just exploring another planet; they are paving the way for the first human footprints in the Martian soil.

  • While modern rovers like Curiosity and Perseverance are the size of an SUV, Sojourner was a tiny pioneer. It weighed only about 23 pounds (10.6 kg) and was roughly 2 feet long. Despite its small size, it was the first wheeled vehicle to ever operate on another planet.
  • Sojourner was an "experimental" mission designed to survive for only 7 Martian days (sols). However, it proved much tougher than expected, staying active and exploring the Martian surface for 83 sols. During that time, it sent back over 550 images and analyzed the chemical composition of rocks with names like "Barnacle Bill" and "Scooby-Doo."
  • Perseverance carries an instrument called MOXIE (Mars Oxygen In-Situ Resource Utilization Experiment). It successfully proved that we can "breathe" on Mars by converting the Martian carbon dioxide atmosphere into pure oxygen. This is a huge deal for your "Contact" or "Future" sections because it’s the technology that will eventually allow astronauts to breathe and create rocket fuel for the trip back home.
  • While previous rovers gave us incredible photos, Perseverance was the first to give us ears on the ground. It is equipped with two microphones that have captured the first-ever audio recordings of the Martian wind, the "pop" of its own lasers hitting rocks, and even the whirring of the Ingenuity helicopter flying nearby.
